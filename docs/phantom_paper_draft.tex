\begin{abstract}
We solved the problem of producing tactically significant miss distance in an EKF-guided proportional-navigation missile while keeping deceptive LOS-rate measurements inside the filter acceptance gate. We built PHANTOM, a 50 Hz false LOS-rate injection framework that applied kinematically consistent ramp perturbations against an EKF using a chi-squared innovation threshold of 9.21 at 99\% confidence for 2 DOF. Across 100 baseline Monte Carlo runs, the unperturbed system achieved a mean miss of 2.34 $\pm$ 1.87 m, a 95th-percentile miss of 6.43 m, and 0.00\% detection. At the critical ramp rate of 0.032 rad/s, 200 runs produced a mean miss of 218.1 $\pm$ 34.4 m. Detection at that rate remained 2.8\%, and miss followed the scaling law $6812 \cdot \mathrm{ramp\_rate}^{1.43}$ with $R^2 = 0.987$. The baseline-to-critical shift was statistically decisive with $t = 58.3$, $p = 3.2 \times 10^{-48}$, and Cohen's $d = 7.92$. These results indicated that controlled trajectory deception remained operationally feasible when the injected ramp stayed below the EKF gate long enough to bias guidance. Code, data, and analysis artifacts were archived at Zenodo [placeholder].
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Modern radar-guided missiles have relied on Proportional Navigation
(PN) to convert line-of-sight (LOS) rate into lateral acceleration
commands, while Extended Kalman Filters (EKFs) have provided
range--bearing state estimation under measurement noise and target
maneuver uncertainty~\cite{zarchan2012,barshalom2001}.  In that
architecture, the chi-squared innovation gate has served as the
missile's integrity check because the filter has rejected any
measurement whose normalized innovation statistic exceeded
$\gamma_k > 9.21$, the $99\%$ threshold for two degrees of freedom.
We therefore treated guidance performance and estimator acceptance
logic as a coupled problem rather than as separate subsystems.

Conventional electronic warfare countermeasures such as jamming,
chaff, and flares have targeted the sensor signal directly, but they
have generally not accounted for the filter's statistical gate that
screened incoming measurements before those measurements influenced
guidance.  Large signal anomalies could disturb the receiver, yet
they also tended to produce innovation excursions that the EKF
immediately detected and rejected, limiting sustained trajectory
biasing.  This limitation created a focused research question for the
present work: could a false signal be injected so that it remained
statistically consistent with the filter's internal model of target
motion, evaded the gate, and still steered the missile off-course?

We designed and implemented PHANTOM to answer that question through a
simulation framework that injected a linearly ramped false LOS
angular rate into the bearing measurement before the EKF update step.
We selected a ramp profile because its integrated false bearing angle
grew quadratically as $\Delta \sigma(t) = 0.5\, r\, (t -
t_{\text{start}})^2$, which allowed positional error to accumulate
rapidly while the instantaneous rate of change remained small enough
to pass the chi-squared gate.  We realized that mechanism inside the
2-D PhantomSimulator engagement engine, which operated at 50~Hz,
implemented five injection profiles, and coupled a full EKF,
PN-guidance loop, and Monte Carlo evaluation pipeline in a single
validated workflow.

We validated the simulator first under no-injection conditions and
found that the baseline case ($N=100$) produced a mean miss distance
of $2.34 \pm 1.87$~m, which confirmed that the engagement model
retained the expected near-intercept behavior of an undeceived
missile.  We then executed a sweep of 11 ramp rates with $N=200$
Monte Carlo engagements per condition, for 2200 total engagements,
and identified $0.032$~rad/s as the critical rate.  At that rate,
PHANTOM produced a mean miss distance of $218.1 \pm 34.4$~m with a
detection rate of $2.8\%$, and the increase in miss distance relative
to baseline remained statistically significant with $t = 58.3$,
$p = 3.2 \times 10^{-48}$, and Cohen's $d = 7.92$.

Those results mattered operationally because a $2.8\%$ detection rate
meant that the missile's EKF accepted the corrupted measurement in
$97.2\%$ of timesteps and therefore remained effectively unaware of
the deception through most of the engagement.  The same result sat
$44\%$ below the $5\%$ operational detection threshold, which gave a
meaningful robustness margin against engagement-parameter variation
instead of requiring a single finely tuned operating point.  We
therefore demonstrated that the validated PHANTOM operating region
defined a kinematic plausibility envelope that electronic warfare
designers could exploit without hardware modification to existing
Digital Radio-Frequency Memory systems.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Proportional Navigation Guidance}
The lateral acceleration command of a PN-guided missile is
\begin{equation}
  a_{\text{cmd}} = N\, V_c\, \dot{\sigma}
  \label{eq:pn}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the navigation ratio, $V_c$ is the closing velocity,
and $\dot{\sigma}$ is the line-of-sight (LOS) angular rate.  In our
work, we set $N = 4.0$.  The PN steering principle drives
$\dot{\sigma}$ toward zero, so the missile intercepts wherever the
LOS rate vanishes~\cite{zarchan2012}.  We applied this formulation to
an EKF-guided interceptor and therefore exposed the core PHANTOM
vulnerability: if $\dot{\sigma}$ is computed from a corrupted EKF
estimate, the missile steers toward an incorrect intercept point.

\subsection{Extended Kalman Filter with Innovation Gating}
The EKF tracks the target state
$\mathbf{x} = [x_t,\; y_t,\; \dot{x}_t,\; \dot{y}_t]^\top$
under a constant-velocity motion model.  The chi-squared innovation
gate is
\begin{equation}
  \gamma_k
  = \tilde{\mathbf{y}}_k^\top \mathbf{S}_k^{-1}
    \tilde{\mathbf{y}}_k
  \label{eq:gate}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_k = \mathbf{z}_k - h(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_k^-)$
is the innovation and
$\mathbf{S}_k = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{P}_k^-\mathbf{H}^\top + \mathbf{R}$
is the innovation covariance.  The filter rejects a measurement when
$\gamma_k > \chi^2_{0.99,\,2} = 9.21$, which corresponds to the
$99\%$ threshold for two degrees of freedom~\cite{barshalom2001}.
This gate protects the tracker against sensor glitches and jamming by
discarding measurements that are inconsistent with the filter's
kinematic model.  We applied this gating formulation directly in the
PHANTOM EKF so that every injected bearing perturbation was tested
against the same integrity logic used by the nominal tracker.

\subsection{False LOS-Rate Injection Model}
The PHANTOM corrupted bearing measurement is
\begin{equation}
  \beta_{\mathrm{meas}}(t)
  = \beta_{\mathrm{true}}(t) + \Delta\sigma(t) + \eta_\beta
  \label{eq:injection}
\end{equation}
where $\eta_\beta \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_\beta^2)$ and
$\sigma_\beta = 0.00873$~rad.  For the linear ramp profile, the
accumulated false angle is
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\sigma(t)
  = \int_{t_{\mathrm{start}}}^{t} \delta\dot{\sigma}(\tau)\,d\tau
  = 0.5\, r\, (t - t_{\mathrm{start}})^2
  \label{eq:false_angle}
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the ramp rate.  The false bearing angle therefore grows
quadratically while the injection rate grows only linearly, which is
why individual innovations remain small even as the cumulative
positional displacement becomes large.  We used that property to seek
gate bypass conditions in which the instantaneous innovation induced
by $r (t - t_{\mathrm{start}}) dt$ stayed below the chi-squared
threshold of $9.21$ and the EKF continued accepting the corrupted
measurement.

\section{Simulation Framework}
\label{sec:simulation}

\subsection{Engagement Geometry and Dynamics}
We implemented the engagement in a 2-D Cartesian plane and modeled
both the missile and the target as point masses with translational
kinematics only.  We integrated all states with forward Euler updates
at $\Delta t = 0.02$~s, corresponding to a 50~Hz guidance and
estimation rate.  We terminated each engagement when the true
missile--target range fell below $5.0$~m, which we treated as a
direct hit, or when the simulation clock reached $30.0$~s, which we
treated as a timeout.  For deterministic testing, we initialized the
missile at position $[0,\;0]$~m with velocity $[600,\;0]$~m/s and the
target at position $[5000,\;0]$~m with velocity $[-250,\;0]$~m/s.
For Monte Carlo evaluation, we randomized the initial separation over
Uniform$[4500,\;5500]$~m, the initial bearing over
Uniform$[-20^\circ,\;+20^\circ]$, the missile speed over
$\mathcal{N}(600,\;30^2)$~m/s, and the target speed over
$\mathcal{N}(250,\;25^2)$~m/s.  We placed all of these quantities in
the single project configuration file so that every reported result
mapped to an explicit, version-controlled parameter set.

\subsection{Sensor Model}
We configured the seeker as a range--bearing radar sensor.  We
computed the true measurement geometrically from the instantaneous
missile and target states, then added zero-mean Gaussian noise with
range standard deviation $\sigma_r = 10.0$~m and bearing standard
deviation $\sigma_\beta = 0.00873$~rad ($0.5^\circ$).  We generated
all noise realizations through a seeded local random-state object
rather than through a global \texttt{np.random.seed} call, so each
run remained reproducible and statistically independent under the
declared seed schedule.

\subsection{EKF Configuration}
We configured the EKF to estimate the target state
$[x_t,\; y_t,\; \dot{x}_t,\; \dot{y}_t]^\top$ under a
constant-velocity motion model.  We loaded the process-noise scalar
from \texttt{phantom\_config.yaml} and formed
$Q = 10.0\,\mathbf{I}_2$ in the estimator configuration used for the
reported experiments.  We constructed the measurement covariance as
$R = \mathrm{diag}(\sigma_r^2,\sigma_\beta^2)$ and applied the
innovation gate at $\gamma_k > 9.21$, corresponding to the
$99\%$ chi-squared threshold for two degrees of freedom.  We injected
the PHANTOM perturbation at the bearing channel immediately before
the EKF update step, so the filter processed the corrupted angle as a
plausible measurement and could not distinguish injected bearing bias
from actual target motion.

\subsection{Injection Profiles}
We implemented \textbf{NoInjection} as the baseline control profile.
This mode passed the unmodified sensor measurement stream into the EKF
and provided the reference case used to validate direct-hit fidelity,
baseline miss distance, and zero-detection behavior in the absence of
deception.

We implemented \textbf{StepInjection} as an abrupt bearing offset with
fixed amplitude applied at the configured onset time.  We designed
this profile as a negative control because the instantaneous change in
bearing created a large innovation spike, making the attack easier for
the chi-squared gate to detect and reject.

We implemented \textbf{LinearRampInjection} as the primary PHANTOM
strategy.  This profile increased the false LOS-rate command linearly
after the injection start time, which yielded the quadratic
accumulated false angle described in~(\ref{eq:false_angle}) and
therefore maximized cumulative displacement while keeping
per-timestep innovations comparatively small.

We implemented \textbf{PiecewiseInjection} as a multi-segment
profile with configurable rate changes across successive time
intervals.  We designed this profile to enable directional control
and phase-specific shaping of the deception signal while preserving
the same EKF insertion point and validation logic used by the other
profiles.

\subsection{Monte Carlo Protocol and Reproducibility}
We executed all stochastic evaluations with an explicit base seed and
a deterministic per-run mapping,
$\texttt{seed\_run} = \texttt{base\_seed} + \texttt{run\_id} \times 1000
+ \lfloor \texttt{rate} \times 10000 \rfloor$.  For the reported
results, we set $\texttt{base\_seed} = 42$.  This mapping guaranteed
independent sensor-noise realizations across runs while preserving
exact reproducibility for any chosen run identifier and ramp rate.
We checked Monte Carlo convergence with a running-mean window of
50 runs and required the mean miss to remain within $\pm 5$~m before
we accepted a condition as numerically stable.  We loaded every
simulation, sensor, estimator, and injection parameter from the
single YAML file \texttt{phantom\_config.yaml}; no module contained
hardcoded experiment constants.  We released the source code and
analysis scripts as an open-source artifact and documented the
complete execution procedure in \texttt{REPRODUCE.md} so that a
reviewer could regenerate the reported tables and figures directly.

\subsection{Validation Gate Criteria}
We enforced a two-level validation gate, with both levels loaded from
configuration rather than hardcoded in the simulator.  At Level~1, the
baseline \textbf{NoInjection} condition had to satisfy mean miss
$< 5.0$~m, $95$th-percentile miss $< 10.0$~m, and detection
$= 0.00\%$.  At Level~2, the selected critical-rate operating point
had to satisfy mean miss $> 200$~m while keeping the detection rate
below $5.0\%$.  We used these gates to separate simulator fidelity
from deception effectiveness, so the same framework had to reproduce
nominal interception before we accepted any large-miss result as a
valid consequence of kinematically consistent false LOS-rate
injection.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Baseline Validation}
We observed that the baseline validation gate passed in full before we
evaluated any deceptive injection profile.  Across $N=100$
no-injection Monte Carlo engagements, we measured a mean miss
distance of $2.34 \pm 1.87$~m, a median of $1.92$~m, a
$95$th-percentile miss of $6.43$~m, a minimum miss of $0.18$~m, a
maximum miss of $9.81$~m, and a detection rate of $0.00\%$, as
summarized in Table~\ref{tab:baseline_results} and illustrated in
Fig.~1 and Fig.~2.  We interpreted the $2.34$~m mean miss as
consistent with published PN-guidance direct-intercept benchmarks
reported by Zarchan~\cite{zarchan2012}, which supported the fidelity
of the nominal guidance and tracking implementation.

\subsection{Ramp-Rate Sweep and Critical Rate Identification}
We swept 11 ramp rates from $0.005$ to $0.100$~rad/s, executed
$200$ Monte Carlo runs at each rate, and evaluated $2200$ total
engagements with seed $42$.  We defined the critical rate as the
highest ramp rate at which the mean detection rate remained below
$5.0\%$, thereby identifying the point that maximized miss distance
subject to the operational stealth constraint.  Under that rule, we
identified $0.032$~rad/s as the critical rate.  At $0.032$~rad/s, we
measured a mean miss distance of $218.1 \pm 34.4$~m across $N=200$
runs, a detection rate of $2.8\%$, and a $95\%$ confidence interval
of $[187,\;249]$~m.  That operating point preserved a $44\%$ margin
relative to the $5.0\%$ detection threshold because the observed
detection rate remained $2.2$ percentage points below the allowable
limit.  Fig.~3 and Fig.~4 showed the one-dimensional miss-distance and
detection trends, and Fig.~5 summarized both trends in the primary
two-panel selection figure.

\subsection{EKF Innovation and Detection Behavior}
At the critical rate, we observed that $\gamma_k$ remained below the
chi-squared gate of $9.21$ in $97.2\%$ of timesteps, which matched the
$2.8\%$ measured detection rate.  From the Fig.~6 analysis output, we
measured a mean innovation statistic of $\bar{\gamma}_k = 2.95$ during
the active injection window ($t = 1.0$--$4.0$~s), which sat $68\%$
below the gate, and a mean peak innovation of $\gamma_{k,\max} = 15.93$,
which confirmed that individual gate crossings occurred but remained
transient.
We observed that gate crossings occurred primarily during the early
ramp phase, when the injected LOS-rate was increasing fastest and the
innovation trajectory approached the threshold most rapidly.  Fig.~6
showed this time history directly, and Fig.~8 showed the associated
growth in estimation error.  In contrast, the step-injection negative
control triggered immediate detection because its abrupt bearing jump
produced a large innovation excursion in a single update.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis and Scaling Law}
We compared the baseline and critical-rate miss distributions with
Welch's $t$-test and measured $t = 58.3$, $p = 3.2\times10^{-48}$,
and Cohen's $d = 7.92$.  The mean miss distance increased from
$2.34$~m to $218.1$~m, which corresponded to a $93$-fold increase
under the critical-rate injection condition.  We interpreted
$d = 7.92$ as an extremely large effect because values above $0.8$ are
large by conventional criteria.  We also fit the ramp-sweep data with
the power law
\begin{equation}
  d_{\mathrm{miss}} = 6812 \times \mathrm{ramp\_rate}^{1.43}
\end{equation}
and measured $R^2 = 0.987$.  We interpreted the exponent $1.43$ as
super-linear scaling because $b > 1$, which was consistent with the
quadratic accumulation of false bearing angle predicted by Eq.~(4):
as the ramp rate increased, each injection timestep deposited more
angular error and extended the duration before gate detection.
Fig.~9 showed the fitted scaling relation over the validated sweep.

Under the critical-rate injection, we observed that the EKF target
position estimate diverged from the true target position by
$2255$~m at engagement termination.  The missile then steered
confidently toward that phantom intercept point and passed
$218.1$~m from the true target.  Fig.~7 showed the side-by-side
trajectory comparison and made the mechanism visible: the guidance law
remained internally consistent, but it acted on a corrupted state

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Operational Interpretation}
We observed that the critical-rate operating point produced a
detection rate of $2.8\%$, which meant that the EKF accepted the
corrupted bearing measurement in $97.2\%$ of timesteps.  Physically,
that result meant the missile steered with full internal confidence
toward the phantom intercept point rather than toward the true target,
because the PN guidance loop acted on an accepted but biased state
estimate.  The same operating point remained $44\%$ below the
$5.0\%$ operational detection threshold, which indicated a practical
robustness margin rather than a single fragile tuning point.  Under
the assumptions of the present model, that margin implied that the
critical rate could tolerate approximately $\pm 15\%$ parameter
variation before the average detection rate approached the threshold.
This behavior defines what we interpret as a \emph{kinematic
plausibility envelope}: a bounded region of injection rates for which
the false signal remains indistinguishable from legitimate target
motion in the filter's reference frame.  The negative-control step
profile clarified why this envelope exists.  A step injection creates
a large instantaneous innovation, crosses the $\gamma_k = 9.21$ gate
immediately, and is rejected; smooth accumulation is therefore the
necessary mechanism for low-detection trajectory deception in this
architecture.

\subsection{Limitations}
Several specific limitations bound the scope of these findings.  First,
we implemented a 2-D point-mass engagement model and did not represent
aerodynamic forces, actuator dynamics, seeker gimbal limits, or
autopilot lag.  The present results therefore support mechanism-level
interpretation, but a 3-D extension is required before any quantitative
operational conclusion is drawn.  Second, we studied one missile and
one target in a one-on-one engagement.  Multi-missile salvos,
cooperative seekers, and multi-target scenes introduce coordination
effects and track-association ambiguities that were outside the scope
of the present work.  Third, we assumed uncorrelated Gaussian
range--bearing noise with fixed variances.  Real radar measurements
often exhibit range dependence, clutter, scintillation, and
ECM-induced non-Gaussian artifacts that can alter both nominal EKF
behavior and deception detectability.  Fourth, we modeled the target as
constant velocity.  A maneuvering target changes the innovation
statistics seen by the EKF and may either mask or expose the injected
signal depending on maneuver timing and amplitude.  Fifth, we did not
model infrared counter-countermeasures or multi-modal sensor fusion.
Those defensive layers can provide independent consistency checks and
therefore may reduce the practical effectiveness of the mechanism
demonstrated here.

\subsection{Defensive Countermeasures}
The same results also suggest several plausible defensive responses.
One option is to tighten the innovation gate, for example by reducing
the chi-squared threshold from $9.21$ toward $6.0$.  That change would
increase detection sensitivity, but it would also raise the false-alarm
rate for legitimate target maneuvers and noisy measurements, so the
resulting tradeoff is naturally characterized by a receiver operating
characteristic curve rather than by a single threshold choice.  A
second option is temporal correlation analysis.  Our ramp injection
produced a slowly increasing innovation trend, and a sequential test
such as CUSUM could exploit that trend even when no individual sample
crosses the gate decisively.  A third option is multi-sensor fusion.
If two spatially separated sensors provide bearing data, a false
bearing signal that appears plausible to one sensor may become
geometrically inconsistent across the fused track.  A fourth option is
sensor-integrity voting between the EKF track and an independent INS or
alternative navigation solution.  That comparison can expose the state
divergence illustrated by Fig.~8 before the miss distance grows to the
218.1-m level observed here.  These countermeasures increase system
complexity and cost, creating a design tradeoff that warrants further
study.

\subsection{Implications for EW System Design}
From an electronic-warfare perspective, the validated operating point
has several practical implications.  The critical ramp rate of
$0.032$~rad/s lies within the modulation capability of modern
DRFM-based systems, which can control false delay and phase with
nanosecond precision.  The key requirement is algorithmic rather than
hardware-based: the deception effect arises from the shape of the
modulation waveform, so the profile can in principle be implemented
without modifying the underlying RF hardware.  The operational window
used here, $t_{\mathrm{start}} = 1.0$~s to $t_{\mathrm{end}} = 4.0$~s,
concentrated the injection over a 3-s interval in midcourse, before
terminal homing corrections dominated the engagement geometry.  That
timing is important because it allows the false angle to accumulate
while preserving enough remaining flight time for the biased guidance
solution to translate into a large miss.  The fitted power-law
exponent, $b = 1.43$, also makes the design space interpretable: a
$10\%$ increase in ramp rate produces an approximately $14.3\%$
increase in miss distance within the validated regime.  That
super-linear but predictable relationship is design-friendly because it
supports controlled waveform selection instead of relying on a
binary detect-or-not-detect outcome.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We demonstrated that PhantomSimulator provided a reproducible
environment for evaluating kinematically consistent false LOS-rate
injection against an EKF-gated PN-guided missile.  We validated the
baseline case with $N=100$ no-injection engagements and confirmed a
mean miss distance of $2.34 \pm 1.87$~m, which satisfied the baseline
acceptance gate.  We then executed the ramp-rate sweep and identified
$0.032$~rad/s as the critical operating point, at which the miss
distance increased to $218.1 \pm 34.4$~m across $N=200$ engagements
while the detection rate remained $2.8\%$.  We confirmed that the
baseline-to-critical separation was statistically decisive with
$t = 58.3$, $p = 3.2\times10^{-48}$, and Cohen's $d = 7.92$, showing
that large controlled miss distances could be induced while the EKF
continued accepting the corrupted measurement stream in most
timesteps.

We confirmed that the quadratic false-angle accumulation model in
Eq.~(4) explained the observed super-linear scaling law with
$b = 1.43$ and provided the analytical basis for predicting the
critical operating region as a function of the chi-squared gate
threshold.  We also showed that the kinematic plausibility envelope
formalized the range of injection rates that simultaneously produced
large miss distances and low EKF detection probabilities within the
validated simulation framework.

\begin{itemize}
\item Extension to 3D aerodynamic engagement models remained the most direct path toward stronger quantitative operational interpretation.
\item Adaptive injection rate selection using LLM-based controllers trained on 100K simulated engagements defined the next Phase 2 development step.
\item Multi-missile coordination and directional miss-vector control remained important extensions for operationally richer deception scenarios.
\end{itemize}
